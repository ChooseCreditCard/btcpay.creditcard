How Merchants Actually Handle Bitcoin & Bitcoin-Card Payments

BTCPay.Creditcard explains merchant-side flows for Bitcoin payments and Bitcoin-linked cards – processors, self-hosted BTCPay setups, conversion to fiat and settlement risk.

Card Rails vs. Native Bitcoin Payments

From a merchant’s point of view, there are two broad ways to accept “Bitcoin” at checkout:

  • Bitcoin-linked cards: The merchant sees a normal card payment in fiat. BTC conversion happens on the customer’s side via the card issuer or partner.
  • Native Bitcoin / Lightning payments: The merchant (or their processor) generates an invoice and receives BTC or fiat when the invoice is paid.

BTCPay-like setups usually refer to the second category: native BTC or Lightning invoices, sometimes combined with optional auto-conversion to fiat.

Typical BTCPay-Style Payment Flow

For native BTC payments, a BTCPay-like flow looks roughly like this:

  • The customer selects “Pay with Bitcoin” or scans a QR code at checkout.
  • The merchant’s payment server generates an invoice with an amount (in fiat or BTC) and a time window.
  • The customer’s wallet sends BTC (on-chain) or a Lightning payment that matches the invoice.
  • The payment server detects settlement, marks the invoice as paid and notifies the merchant system.
  • Optionally, BTC is auto-converted to fiat via an exchange integration.

For Bitcoin-linked cards, the flow is almost identical to any other card transaction – the merchant’s acquirer settles fiat, while BTC is handled elsewhere.

Settlement Choices: Keep BTC or Convert to Fiat

Merchants using BTCPay-style setups have to decide how much BTC they want to hold versus convert:

  • Full BTC settlement: Merchant keeps 100% of incoming BTC. Highest exposure to volatility, but also highest upside if BTC appreciates.
  • Partial auto-conversion: A portion (e.g. 50%) is automatically sold to fiat, while the rest remains in BTC.
  • Full fiat conversion: BTC is sold immediately, merchant receives only fiat. Reduces volatility but may incur spreads and exchange fees.

Each option has implications for treasury management, accounting and tax.

Fees, Spreads and Operational Overhead

Accepting Bitcoin natively introduces new cost centres compared to pure card acceptance:

  • Network fees (on-chain) or routing fees (Lightning).
  • Spreads and trading fees when converting BTC to fiat.
  • Extra monitoring, reconciliation and potential wallet management.
  • Implementation and maintenance of self-hosted payment servers.

For some merchants, the marketing value of “we accept Bitcoin” may outweigh these frictions; for others, pure card acceptance via Bitcoin-linked cards is simpler.

Chargebacks, Finality and Customer Protection

Card payments come with chargeback rules – sometimes painful for merchants, but also familiar and structured. Native Bitcoin payments are typically final once confirmed, unless the merchant voluntarily issues a refund.

This changes how disputes are handled. Merchants must adapt policies and customer communication accordingly, especially if they mix BTC and card methods.

Comparing Merchant Bitcoin Acceptance Models

Model Merchant Sees Key Trade-Off
Bitcoin-Linked Card Only Normal card payments in fiat Easy to support, no BTC handling – but limited “we accept BTC” signal.
Custodial BTC Processor Dashboard with BTC & fiat balances Simpler UX, but processor holds funds and controls keys.
Self-Hosted BTCPay-Style Server Direct BTC / Lightning receipts Maximum control, but requires technical skills and ops capacity.
Hybrid: BTC + Card BTC invoices + classic cards side by side More options for customers, more complexity for accounting.
